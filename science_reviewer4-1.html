📚 Chapter Summary: An invention is a new device, method, or concept created to solve a problem, and a patent is a government license that protects an inventor's work from being copied. 📚 Chapter Summary: Physical properties are characteristics of a material that can be observed or measured without changing what the material is made of — things like hardness, brittleness, malleability, elasticity, melting point, and boiling point.
PropertyMeaningExample
💎 HardnessAbility to resist scratchingDiamond
💥 BrittlenessTendency to break, crack, or snap easilyGraphite (pencil lead), glass
🥇 MalleabilityEase of being hammered or rolled into thin sheetsGold (most malleable metal)
🎗️ ElasticityReturns to original shape after stretching or bendingRubber band, spring
🧊 Melting PointTemperature a solid turns into a liquidIce → 0°C
💨 Boiling PointTemperature a liquid turns into a gasWater → 100°C
📌 REMEMBER Don't confuse brittleness (breaks easily) with elasticity (bounces back) — they describe opposite reactions to force! And always double-check: ice melts at 0°C, water boils at 100°C.
🧠 Memory Trick "A Hard rock is Brittle, but Malleable gold bends and Elastic rubber snaps back — until it Melts or Boils away!" The bolded letters spell out H-B-M-E-M-B, the six physical properties in order.
📚 Chapter Summary: Chemical properties only become observable when a material undergoes a chemical reaction and turns into something new — unlike physical properties, you can't see a chemical property just by looking at or handling the material.
PropertyMeaningExample
🔥 CombustibilityTendency to ignite and sustain burningWood, paper
⛽ FlammabilityAbility to burn in the presence of oxygenGasoline, kerosene
⚗️ ReactivityReacts with another substance to form something newBaking soda + vinegar
🦠 CorrosionHappens when iron reacts with oxygen, producing rustRusty nail
☠️ ToxicityDegree to which a material can harm a living organismPesticides, cleaners
🍂 BiodegradabilityDecomposes due to microorganismsFruit peelings, dried leaves
📌 REMEMBER If a material only changes when it reacts and becomes something new (like burning, rusting, or rotting), that's a chemical property. If you can observe it without any reaction (like color or shape), that's physical.
🧠 Memory Trick Picture a trash can: things that burn (combustibility, flammability), things that rust or react (corrosion, reactivity), things that are dangerous (toxicity), and things that rot away naturally (biodegradability). Four buckets, six words — much easier to sort!
