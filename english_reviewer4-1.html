📚 Chapter Summary: A noun is singular if it names only one, and plural if it names more than one. Regular nouns form their plural simply by adding -s or -es. Nouns ending in -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, and -z take -es (bus → buses). Some nouns ending in -o take -s, -es, or both (tomato → tomatoes, piano → pianos). Nouns ending in -y preceded by a consonant change the y to i before adding -es (city → cities), while those preceded by a vowel just add -s (boy → boys). Many nouns ending in -f or -fe change the f to v before adding -es or -s (leaf → leaves, wife → wives).
RuleExample
🔤 Add -s to most nounsgirl → girls, idea → ideas
➕ Add -es to nouns ending -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -zbus → buses, wish → wishes, fox → foxes
🍅 Nouns ending in -o: add -s or -estomato → tomatoes, piano → pianos
🏙️ -y after consonant → change to -ies; after vowel → add -scity → cities, boy → boys
🍃 -f / -fe → change to -v, add -es or -sleaf → leaves, wife → wives
📌 REMEMBER Watch out for words like quiz → quizzes, where the final letter is doubled before adding -es — and for -o words like zero and volcano, which can take either -s or -es.
🧠 Memory Trick "Buzzy Foxes Wish for Watches" — nouns ending in -s, -x, -sh, -ch, -z all take -es, just like the words in that sentence!
📚 Chapter Summary: Irregular nouns do not follow the simple -s or -es rule. Some change their vowel letters (man → men, foot → feet). Some change spelling altogether (mouse → mice, child → children). Some keep the same spelling in both singular and plural (deer → deer, sheep → sheep). Some nouns are always plural in form and meaning (pants, scissors, eyeglasses). And some words borrowed from other languages follow their original language's plural rule (cactus → cacti, criterion → criteria, bacterium → bacteria).
CategoryExamples
🔁 Vowel changes within the wordman → men, foot → feet, woman → women, tooth → teeth
✏️ Spelling changes altogethermouse → mice, child → children, ox → oxen, die → dice
🦌 No change neededdeer → deer, sheep → sheep, shrimp → shrimp, swine → swine
👖 Always plural in form & meaningpants, scissors, eyeglasses, clothes
🌍 Borrowed words, foreign plural rulescactus → cacti, criterion → criteria, bacterium → bacteria
📌 REMEMBER Irregular nouns don't follow one single rule — the safest way to be sure of their plural form is to memorize them or check a dictionary, especially for words borrowed from Latin or Greek.
🧠 Memory Trick "Feet and Teeth Chase Mice and Geese" — a silly sentence to help you remember that these common irregular plurals change their vowels or spelling completely.
📚 Chapter Summary: Nouns can also be classified as a count noun or a mass noun. A count noun can be counted one by one, like house, window, or device. A mass noun cannot be counted, or is difficult to count, like information, water, or rice — instead, we measure or describe it in general amounts.

📌 REMEMBER If you can put a number in front of it and make it plural (one book, two books), it's a count noun. If you can't, like one jewelry or two cashes, it's a mass noun.
🧠 Memory Trick Count it? = Count Noun. Can't count it? = Mass Noun. Ask yourself: "Can I say 'two' of it?" If yes, it's countable!
